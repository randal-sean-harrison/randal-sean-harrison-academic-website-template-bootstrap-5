My Research

The research I conduct takes on very different shapes across the range of areas I work in. As a critical rhetorician, Public Sphere Analysis names my theory and method. My work on political discourse and social movements sticks closer to the context and lived experiences of the participants I examine, but recognizes the imbricated nature of both power and persuasion, as well. Apropos of the Frankfurt School, interdisciplinary methods, including a variety of critical discourse analysis and ethnographic approaches, appeal to me.

My interests run through these areas in the fields of communication and social science:

  • Rhetorical criticism, social movement studies, political communication, digital activism
  • Media effects, audience studies, critical theory, health communication

Dissertation

The Rhetoric of Repair: Reconstituting Trust, Authority, and Publics in Post-Crisis Digital Spaces

This dissertation investigates the rhetorical strategies employed by institutions (governments, media outlets, and corporations) in the aftermath of large-scale public crises, such as health pandemics or democratic shocks. Drawing upon critical rhetoric and public sphere theory, it develops the concept of "Rhetoric of Repair" to analyze how communicative acts attempt to reconstitute public trust, reaffirm institutional authority, and re-stabilize fractured public spheres in digital environments. Employing a multi-modal critical discourse analysis across traditional press releases, social media campaigns, and deepfake counter-messaging, this study identifies three primary rhetorical postures—confession, deflection, and prefiguration—and maps their efficacy in fostering or frustrating civic engagement. Ultimately, this work argues that the success of institutional repair rhetoric hinges not on transparency, but on the strategic use of narrative temporality to frame the past as remediable, the present as responsible, and the future as unified.
