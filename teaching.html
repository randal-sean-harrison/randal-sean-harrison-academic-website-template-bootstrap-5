Teaching

From analyzing the rhetoric of policy to dissecting the ethical challenges of communication infrastructure, my courses are often focused by urgent public questions, including but not limited to those raised by the spread of disinformation and the precarity of democratic institutions. I challenge students to critically interrogate the discursive formations that shape public opinion and political action, and I leverage a strong background in rhetorical criticism, critical theory, and qualitative methods to give students real-world analytic experience, where appropriate. For more on my pedagogical approach, please see my Teaching Philosophy.

Courses Taught

This graduate seminar introduces core methodologies of Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA), focusing on how language constructs, maintains, and challenges social power structures. We examine texts and interactions across political, media, and institutional contexts, analyzing the relationship between discourse, ideology, and hegemony. Students will apply models from Fairclough, Van Dijk, and Wodak. Goals: Master advanced textual analysis; critique ideological structures; develop CDA-based research proposals.

This course explores the crucial role of rhetoric in managing public health crises and negotiating scientific authority. Topics include vaccine hesitancy, pandemic communication, environmental discourse, and the interplay between specialized knowledge and public understanding. The class investigates how metaphors, narratives, and risk communication shape public trust in experts and institutions. Goals: Analyze crisis communication; evaluate scientific narratives; design effective public health messages.

An exploration of how digital platforms (Twitter, Telegram, TikTok) are utilized by activists to mobilize, organize, and frame social and political protests globally. The course applies rhetorical theory to understand networked collective action, platform governance, and the challenges of slacktivism versus sustained engagement. It critically examines the affordances and limitations of online organizing.
Goals: Critique platform effects; analyze movement rhetoric; develop digital campaign strategies.

This class extends traditional theories of media effects (cultivation, framing) to the contemporary digital environment, focusing on the influence of algorithmic curation and artificial intelligence on user behavior and political polarization. We investigate how platform design creates filter bubbles and exacerbates societal biases across multiple domains.
Goals: Compare classic and digital effects theories; assess algorithmic power; propose platform accountability mechanisms.

This course investigates the persuasive function of collective memory, examining how monuments, museums, anniversaries, and historical narratives are rhetorically constructed to shape national identity and historical consensus. We study ongoing controversies over commemoration and analyze counter-memory efforts by marginalized communities to challenge dominant narratives.
Goals: Interpret symbolic memorials; apply memory theories to current debates; conduct historical rhetorical research.

A deep dive into the strategic deployment of narrative in modern political campaigns, governance, and international relations. The course covers mythic structures, character archetypes, framing, and storytelling techniques used by political actors to persuade audiences, build coalitions, and define opponents. This course prepares students to analyze political messages at a high level.
Goals: Deconstruct political narratives; evaluate strategic framing; apply narrative theory to policy analysis.
